Myanmar at a Glance

An introduction to one of Southeast Asia's most diverse countries

Myanmar, formerly known as Burma, is a sovereign state in Southeast Asia bordered by Bangladesh, India, China, Laos and Thailand. With a population of about 54 million and a territory of 676,578 square kilometers, it is the largest country in Mainland Southeast Asia and the 26th most populous country in the world.

Myanmar is a land of incredible diversity, both in its geography - from snow-capped mountains in the north to pristine beaches along the western coastline - and in its people, with 135 distinct ethnic groups officially recognized by the government, each with their own customs, languages, and traditions.

After decades of isolation under military rule, Myanmar has been gradually opening up to the outside world since 2011, revealing to visitors its unspoiled landscapes, well-preserved ancient sites, and a culture largely unaffected by globalization.

Terrain

Myanmar features diverse geography with central lowlands surrounded by steep, rugged highlands. The country can be divided into five distinct regions: the northern mountains, western ranges, the eastern plateau, the central basin, and the coastal strip.

Mountain Ranges

The northern highlands include some of Southeast Asia's highest mountains, with Hkakabo Razi in Kachin State standing at 5,881 meters (19,295 feet) as the highest point in Myanmar. The Arakan Yoma mountain range in the west forms a barrier between Myanmar and the Indian subcontinent.

Rivers

The Irrawaddy (Ayeyarwady) River is Myanmar's longest river at 2,170 km (1,348 miles). It forms a vast delta before emptying into the Andaman Sea. Other major rivers include the Chindwin, Salween (Thanlwin), and Sittaung rivers.

Climate

Myanmar has a tropical monsoon climate with three distinct seasons:

  • Cool Season (November-February): Dry and relatively cool with average temperatures between 20-24°C (68-75°F)
  • Hot Season (March-May): Very hot and dry with temperatures often exceeding 40°C (104°F) in central regions
  • Rainy Season (June-October): Southwest monsoon bringing heavy rainfall, particularly to coastal and delta regions

Biodiversity

Myanmar is recognized as one of the most biodiverse countries in Southeast Asia. It contains pristine forests, extensive wetlands, and coral reefs that support a rich variety of plant and animal species, including endangered species like tigers, elephants, and Irrawaddy dolphins.

Historical Timeline

A journey through Myanmar's rich and complex history

1st-9th Century

Early Kingdoms

Various city-states and kingdoms flourished, including the Pyu city-states and the Mon kingdoms in Lower Burma. The Pyu civilization was centered around the city of Sri Ksetra.

1044-1287

Pagan Kingdom

King Anawrahta established the first Burmese Empire at Pagan (Bagan). The kingdom unified regions that would later form modern Myanmar for the first time. Buddhism flourished with thousands of temples built across the Bagan plains.

14th-16th Century

Divided Kingdoms

After the fall of Pagan to Mongol invasions, Myanmar fragmented into several smaller kingdoms, including the Ava Kingdom, Hanthawaddy Kingdom, and Shan States.

1510-1752

Toungoo Dynasty

The Toungoo Dynasty reunified Myanmar and created the largest empire in Southeast Asia's history, including parts of modern Thailand, Laos, and China's Yunnan province.

1752-1885

Konbaung Dynasty

The last Burmese royal dynasty ruled from 1752 until its overthrow by the British in 1885. King Alaungpaya founded the dynasty. His grandson, King Bodawpaya, built the world's largest brick pagoda, Mingun Pahtodawgyi.

1824-1885

British Colonial Period

Britain conquered Burma through three Anglo-Burmese Wars (1824-1826, 1852, and 1885). By 1886, the entire country became a province of British India, ending the Burmese monarchy.

1942-1945

World War II & Japanese Occupation

Japan occupied Burma during World War II with the initial assistance of the Burma Independence Army led by Aung San. Later, the BIA switched allegiance to the Allies.

1948

Independence

Burma gained independence from British rule on January 4, 1948, after negotiations led by General Aung San, who was assassinated in 1947 before independence was achieved.

1962-2011

Military Rule

General Ne Win led a coup in 1962, establishing military rule that lasted for nearly 50 years. The country became increasingly isolated under the "Burmese Way to Socialism" and later under the State Law and Order Restoration Council (SLORC).

1989

Name Change

The military government officially changed the country's English name from "Burma" to "Myanmar" and renamed many cities, including Rangoon to Yangon.

2011-Present

Political Reforms & Recent Developments

A nominally civilian government took power in 2011, initiating a series of political and economic reforms. The country held relatively free elections in 2015, with Aung San Suu Kyi's National League for Democracy winning a landslide victory. However, in February 2021, the military staged another coup, returning the country to military rule.

Culture & Society

Exploring Myanmar's rich cultural tapestry and traditions

Religion

Theravada Buddhism is the predominant religion, practiced by approximately 88% of the population. The country's landscape is dotted with golden pagodas and monasteries, with monastic education playing a significant role in society. Other religions practiced include Christianity, Islam, Hinduism, and various indigenous beliefs.

Ethnic Diversity

Myanmar officially recognizes 135 distinct ethnic groups, with the Bamar (Burman) making up about 68% of the population. Other major groups include the Shan, Karen, Rakhine, Chin, Kachin, Mon, and Kayah. Each group maintains its own cultural identity, language, and traditions.

Languages

Burmese (Myanmar language) is the official language and is spoken by the majority of the population. However, there are over 100 languages and dialects spoken throughout the country, reflecting its ethnic diversity. Many ethnic languages belong to the Tibeto-Burman, Mon-Khmer, and Tai-Kadai language families.

Traditional Arts

Myanmar has a rich tradition of arts and crafts, including lacquerware, wood and stone carving, bronze casting, silversmithing, and puppetry. The Yama Zatdaw (Ramayana) is the basis for much of Myanmar's classical dance and drama. Traditional music uses unique instruments like the saung (harp), pat waing (drum circle), and hne (oboe).

Customs & Etiquette

Myanmar society is known for its politeness and respect for elders. Traditional greetings involve a slight bow. When visiting religious sites, shoulders and knees must be covered, and shoes removed. The traditional costume is the longyi, a sarong-like garment worn by both men and women. Thanaka, a yellowish-white paste made from ground bark, is applied to the face as both cosmetic and sunscreen.

Festivals

Thingyan (Water Festival) marking the Myanmar New Year in April is the most famous festival, involving water throwing to symbolize cleansing. Other important festivals include Thadingyut (Festival of Lights), Tazaungdaing (Festival of Lights), and numerous pagoda festivals throughout the year. Festivals typically include music, dance, food, and religious ceremonies.

Economy & Resources

Understanding Myanmar's economic landscape and natural wealth

Economic Overview

Myanmar's economy is one of the least developed in Southeast Asia, though it has significant growth potential due to its strategic location, natural resources, and young workforce. After decades of isolation and centralized planning, the country began economic reforms in 2011, including currency exchange rate unification, tax reforms, and foreign investment liberalization.

Key Industries

  • Agriculture: Employs about 70% of the workforce and accounts for approximately 38% of GDP. Main crops include rice, beans, pulses, sesame, and sugarcane. Myanmar is a major rice exporter.
  • Natural Resources: Rich in petroleum, timber, tin, antimony, zinc, copper, tungsten, lead, coal, limestone, precious stones, natural gas, and hydropower.
  • Manufacturing: Primarily focused on processing agricultural products and producing basic consumer goods, textiles, and construction materials.
  • Services: Growing sector including tourism, telecommunications, banking, and retail.

Challenges & Opportunities

Despite its rich resources, Myanmar faces significant economic challenges including inadequate infrastructure, limited access to electricity, underdeveloped financial markets, and political instability. However, the country's strategic location between China, India, and Southeast Asia, abundant natural resources, and growing labor force present substantial opportunities for economic development.

Tourism in Myanmar

Exploring one of Southeast Asia's most authentic destinations

After decades of isolation, Myanmar has emerged as one of Southeast Asia's most intriguing travel destinations. With ancient temples, pristine beaches, diverse landscapes, and a culture largely untouched by globalization, it offers travelers a glimpse into traditional ways of life that have changed little over centuries.

Iconic Landmarks

From the golden splendor of Shwedagon Pagoda in Yangon to the ancient temples of Bagan, Myanmar's architectural wonders span centuries of history and artistic achievement.

Explore Destinations

Unique Experiences

Float over ancient temples in a hot air balloon, witness the unique leg-rowing fishermen of Inle Lake, or explore remote villages where traditional ways of life continue unchanged.

Discover Activities

Culinary Adventures

Myanmar cuisine blends influences from neighboring countries with unique local flavors. Try mohinga (fish noodle soup), tea leaf salad, and a variety of curries and noodle dishes.

Explore Cuisine

Travel Tips

  • Best Time to Visit: November to February offers the most pleasant weather with dry conditions and moderate temperatures.
  • Visa Requirements: Most nationalities require a visa, which can be obtained online through the e-Visa system or at Myanmar embassies and consulates.
  • Currency: The local currency is the Kyat (MMK). While major hotels and some restaurants in tourist areas may accept credit cards, Myanmar remains largely a cash economy.
  • Connectivity: Mobile coverage has improved significantly, with SIM cards readily available for tourists. However, internet access can be slow and unreliable in remote areas.
  • Cultural Sensitivity: Myanmar is a conservative country with deep religious traditions. Dress modestly, particularly when visiting religious sites, and be respectful of local customs.
